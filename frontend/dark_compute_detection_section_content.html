Predicting the likelihood of detection

We don't directly predict the likelihood of 'detection' at each time step. Instead, we model how much confidence US intelligence has that a covert PRC AI project exists. Once this confidence exceeds some threshold (we chose a x increase in odds), then a project is 'detected,' after which the US exits the bilateral AI slowdown agreement.

The confidence US intelligence agencies gain for the existence of a covert project comes from two sources: discrepancies between reported and estimated quantities of semiconductor assets, and direct observations of covert operations (via IMGINT, HUMINT, SIGINT, etc).
—
At the start of an AI slowdown agreement, the PRC government reports quantities of key assets, such as its AI chip stock and semiconductor manufacturing equipment (SME). If these reported quantities are lower than the quantities US intelligence estimates the PRC has, then this discrepancy provides evidence that assets were diverted to a covert project.

How did we model intelligence gathering? We used two main pieces of unclassified historical data: (1) how well intelligence agencies estimated the quantities of weapon stockpiles (which are analogous to SME or chip stockpiles), and (2) how quickly intelligence agencies detected covert nuclear operations.
Historical accuracy of intelligence estimates of military asset stockpiles, which we used to predict the errors of US intelligence estimates of PRC chip and SME quantities.
Historical detection speed of covert nuclear operations, which we used to model direct observations of covert activity.
