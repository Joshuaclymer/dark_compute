Introduction

We’ve upgraded our timelines and takeoff models![1] The new unified model is both more realistic (according to us) and better-documented-and-explained.

We divide future AI progress into three stages:

  • Stage 1: Our model uses average-coder-dominating AI (AC-95) as an anchor for forecasting coding automation.
  • Stage 2: Coding and experiment selection[2] skills increase up to the level of a superhuman AI researcher (SAR) at which point human researchers are essentially obsolete.
  • Stage 3: The SAR self-improves, but faces diminishing returns; this could end up in a days-months long takeoff to wildly superhuman AIs, or a years-long takeoff requiring greatly increased training compute.

We may refer to the length of Stage 1 and 2 as “AI timelines,”[3] and Stage 3 as “AI takeoff.”[4] At present, the takeoff behavior is more rough than timelines; we intend to spend more time on takeoff before publication.

Our model tries to predict the AI software R&D capability progression through all of these stages. It’s pretty ambitious, and will probably be wrong in many ways,[5] but we are excited to share it.

Model behavior short summary

See here for a complete mathematical explanation of the model, and here for a model description which starts with a simple model then adds complexities.

Basic setup. We model the progression of effective compute E(t), which is defined as training compute Ctrain(t) multiplied by software efficiency S(t). Ctrain(t) is provided as an input to the model.

Software research effort. We model software research effort RE(t), which accumulates over time into research stock RS(t).[6] Each time RS doubles, S doubles r times (where r is an input parameter).[7]

RE(t) is the product of:

  1. Experiment selection skill T(E(t)) (roughly the value you get per experiment; humans are assumed to have a lognormal distribution, and we aggregate across humans by averaging).
  2. Experiment throughput X(t) (roughly how many experiments you can run).

Experiment throughput. Experiment throughput is a constant elasticity of substitution (CES) function, which means that taking either serial coding labor LC(t) or experiment compute Cexp to infinity would not result in infinite research effort. Initially, coding labor is predominantly contributed by humans.

METR trend extrapolation to set coding automation requirement. We set an effective compute requirement EAA for the coding automation anchor (AA) by extrapolating the METR time horizon trend with present effective compute doubling requirement 𝜏p, doubling difficulty growth factor d,[8] and required time horizon HAA. We optionally include an effective compute gap Egap between the time horizon requirement and the coding automation anchor.

Coding automation behavior. When AA is achieved, AIs have automated 100% of tasks at least as efficiently as the average human coder. We interpolate the automation fraction linearly in terms of log(E) between the current effective compute level and present day.

Experiment selection automation. At the automation anchor AIs have experiment selection skill level TAA, expressed in SDs relative to the median AGI company researcher. AIs’ experiment selection skill improves at a rate of Trate SDs per OOM of effective compute. AIs raise the floor of human experiment selection, raising all less capable humans up to their level (and they continue to improve above top human level).

Model description short summary

One way to understand our model, especially the portion which predicts coding automation, is as a series of modifications to a very simple model.

The very simple model is: Make a guess about what horizon length on an extended version of METR’s horizon length dataset would correspond to the coding automation anchor (AC-95) milestone being achieved in practice. Then, use a straightforward exponential extrapolation of the existing data points to predict when this milestone will be achieved. (this is expanded upon here).

However, this misses out on a bunch of important factors that we want to take into account.

The most important reasons it could be slower, in our opinion:

  • Input growth will slow: Recent progress has been driven to a large extent by exponentially scaling up inputs, most notably compute, but also data[9] and human labor. However, this can’t go on forever, and there are reasons to think it’ll taper off in the next few years as tech companies run into limits of how much they can spend, how quickly they can build new fabs, etc.

Here are the most important reasons it could be faster, in our opinion and according to our sensitivity analysis:

  • Superexponential time horizon growth in effective compute: Even if AI companies were banned from automating AI R&D, our best guess is that the horizon length doubling time should get faster over time, and potentially the horizon should eventually ‘go to infinity.’ This is because once AIs are better at general agency skills than humans, they might be better at every task length. This is confusing to many people (including us) and takes some work to explain, which we’ll do below [todo; this hasn’t yet been written].
  • AI R&D speedups from pre-ACD AIs: Before there are AIs which can fully automate AI R&D, there will be AIs which can partially automate it. As AIs get more capable – for example as METR horizon lengths increase – the acceleration effect should kick in, slowly at first and then faster, causing the trend to curve upwards.

These are just the most important factors (according to us), for predicting coding automation. We attempt to model all of them, plus many more, with this model. See a more thorough summary of which factors we take into account below.

As AI capabilities increase past AC-95, experiment selection automation and diminishing returns become the most important factors.

In order to forecast the arrival time of capability milestones, we need to answer 2 questions:

  1. How quickly will the inputs (i.e. compute and labor) to AI progress advance?
  2. At what point do the cumulative inputs result in enough progress to reach a capability milestone?

Simplest: Extrapolate an exponential time horizon trend

We use METR’s software engineering time horizon benchmark as a capabilities measure. It’s chosen because (a) it’s a coding benchmark (b) that has an established trend from effective compute to capabilities and (c) the trend can at least in theory be extrapolated to very high levels of performance.

We will mostly discuss the coding automation anchor (AA) in this writeup. This is defined as being able to accomplish all categories of coding tasks at least as well as the average AGI company programmer (more details here); the definition is chosen because it slots nicely into our modeling of coding automation (discussed below). We also care about predicting the superhuman coder (SC) arrival date, which is a higher capability level than the AA. Below we describe how we determine when SC arrives.

 

We set the time horizon coding automation anchor (AA) requirement HAA to be the 80% time horizon[10] as measured by the tasks in the METR suite, but assuming that it is extended to tasks much harder than the current maximum of ~8 hours and also that tasks with ambiguities/bugs are removed.[11] This means that at horizon HAA, the AI succeeds 80% of the time; we impose a constraint that the AI must accomplish tasks as efficiently as human researchers.[12][13]

This simple method says that the time horizon trend will grow exponentially over time, until it reaches the automation anchor horizon requirement.

The assumption that this method is making is that any change in the trends in inputs to AI progress (i.e. compute and labor trends) will be cancelled out by a corresponding change in the time horizon trend. For example it could be that the inputs go up at the same exponential rate, and the time horizon is exponential if inputs are increased exponentially. But it could be that the inputs go up more slowly over time, but the trend would have gone superexponential with the same input increase rate, so this cancels out.

Improve the estimate of the required calendar time for SC

Allow time horizon growth modes besides exponential

We next allow time horizon growth to be either faster or slower than exponential growth, in terms of calendar time.

We might expect superexponential growth because of the “general long-horizon agency skills” argument: once an AI can accomplish tasks that take humans a very long time (e.g. in the extreme, 1,000 years), it is likely achieving these with general long-horizon agency skills that are very close to allowing it to do tasks that take 10,000 years; i.e. it now has the ability to decompose hard problems, reliably accomplish sub-tasks, error correct, etc.[14] 

One reason we might expect subexponential growth is data bottlenecks; it may be hard to collect high-quality data for long-horizon tasks. This could instead be modeled as having data as an explicit input to AI progress, but our model doesn’t do this at the moment.

The way this is implemented is via a doubling difficulty growth factor, d, which gets multiplied by the doubling time requirement for each successive doubling time. d=1 implies exponential growth, d<1 implies superexponential growth, d>1 implies subexponential growth. The present doubling time is denoted at 𝜏p.

Allow a calendar time gap on top of the SC time horizon requirement

One can attempt to model the gaps between the METR time horizon benchmark and real world software engineering automation by increasing the required time horizon, but that might not be sufficient or natural if the intention is to model dynamics that the benchmark extrapolation isn’t capturing.

Therefore, we allow a parameter for how difficult it is to reach AA, above and beyond the specified time horizon threshold HAA. When working in units of calendar time, this would mean adding a fixed time length to the time AA is reached.

Model the rate of progress of training compute and software improvements

Take into account changes in training compute growth

In order to take into account changes in the inputs to AI progress, we need to operate in units besides calendar time. The first step is to take into account changes in the training compute growth trend.

Therefore, we can replace calendar time with training compute as an input to time horizon growth. We define Ctrain(t) as the size of the largest training run at time t, and:

C̅train(t) = log10(Ctrain(t))

We specify the training compute time series as a model input (see below for more).

𝜏p is now the increase in C̅train(t) required to double time horizon rather than the increase in calendar time. tAA is now set as the time at which training compute reaches C̅train,AA.

Take into account variation in human labor and experiment compute

Use effective compute to model changes in the rate of software progress

We define effective compute as: the amount of compute that would be required using the software (i.e. algorithms) available at time tref, in order to build the frontier AI systems available at time t.[15] We choose tref=2025, but the choice of tref doesn’t change the capability progression.

We define effective compute E(t) as Ctrain(t) * S(t), where S(t) is the software efficiency.

This means that the time horizon requirement is also ; the only change necessary from the formula laid out above is replacing C̅train with E̅AA = log10(EAA).[16]

We calculate effective compute growth rate (in OOMs per year) as the sum of the growth rate of training compute and software efficiency.

The original AI 2027 timelines model used the very simple gsoftware=[2024 rate] through 2028, then gsoftware=0.5*[2024 rate] afterward (before accounting for automation), based on a projection that human labor growth would slow down.

Model software research effort and its relation to software progress, taking into account coding labor as well as diminishing returns

We define software research effort at timestep t RE(t) as the amount of effort that is currently being put into software R&D, in the sense that if we 2x the research effort, then it will take 2x less time to achieve the same capability level holding training compute constant. [TODO improve definition?]

The simplest way to model software research effort would be to take the human coding labor LC,H(t) and take it an exponent 𝛼 < 1 to convert from parallel to serial labor conversion and incorporate compute bottlenecks.

This was how things worked in our May 2025 version.

Once we are explicitly modeling research effort, we need to model diminishing returns to such effort, i.e. that once more effort has been expended the same amount of research effort results in a smaller software efficiency improvement. This is due to the best research ideas being exhausted, and in the limit research effort should yield no improvement due to physical limits.

The way we model diminishing returns to software research effort is:

  1. We accumulate RE(t) over time to get a total research stock RS(t), that represents the sum of all research effort up through time t.
  2. To model diminishing returns, we assume that each doubling of RS(t) results in a fixed multiplier on software efficiency. In particular, a parameter r is set such that each doubling of RS results in a 2r software efficiency multiplier.[17]
  1. This is the same semi-endogenous "software law of motion" as Davidson's FTM.

For the precise math, see our Overleaf Appendix D.

Model experiment compute

In addition to modeling coding labor, we can also model experiment compute, as a key input to software progress. We call the combination of coding labor and experiment compute experiment throughput, since it roughly represents the ability to run more experiments.

We for now will say that simply RE(t) = experiment throughput at time t = X(t).

The simplest way to model experiment throughput is via a Cobb-Douglas production function:

Cobb-Douglas functions have the property that as you take one of the inputs goes to infinity (in this case, coding labor or experiment compute), the output (in this case, research effort) goes to infinity. We’d prefer for the output to bottleneck below infinity; for example, taking coding labor to infinity doesn't take research effort to infinity because you can implement experiments instantly, but still have limited compute with which to run them.

However, it should result in less than this due to the compute cost of experiments going up as model size goes up.

We supply a time series estimate for experiment compute over time; more info here. The serial coding labor is calculated via a specified human labor time series, plus a parallelization penalty (we discuss coding automation later).

Model experiment selection skill

The other ingredient besides experiment throughput in AI software R&D is the value one gets per experiment. Experiment selection skill represents the ability to propose and select better experiments to carry out, resulting in higher value per experiment.

We model aggregate experiment selection skill T(E(t)) as a multiplier on experiment throughput.

We aggregate experiment selection skill by averaging the skills of all of the company researchers, and set the skill distribution such that the average is 1 before automation.

Therefore set the distribution of experiment selection skills of humans working at a frontier AI company to be lognormally distributed with mean 1. The variance is determined by the requirement that 99.9th percentile skill exceed median skill by a “median to top experiment selection multiplier” parameter.

Model automation

Model automation via AI software R&D uplift

The simplest way to model the automation of AI progress is via multiplying by AI software R&D uplift, which in previous work we’ve referred to as a “progress multiplier.” This represents how much AI automation is speeding up software R&D.

More precisely with this model’s nomenclature uplift U(t) a multiplier on research effort.

This was used in our original timelines and takeoff models.[19]

In the case of the timelines model, it was a conceptual issue because the uplift is dependent on experiment compute and human labor. For example, if you fire 90% of your human workforce at a time where your company is fully automated, your research effort should stay roughly the same, rather than decreasing (due to the uplift being applied to a lower value). In the case of the takeoff model, it was okay because we were only varying AI labor, keeping these other variables constant.

Model automation of coding labor

To fix the conceptual issues with multiplying by uplift, we need to model the automation of each type of labor separately.

To take into account coding labor automation, we model the fraction of coding task categories automated A(E) as a function of effective compute.

To set A, we currently assume linear mapping from OOMs of effective compute to fraction of categories automated.

  1. At AA, it should equal 100%, i.e. the AI has the capability to perform every type of coding task as well as the average company coder who does that task.
  2. Today, the parallel coding multiplier[20] should equal an estimated parameter.

Each category of task has a conversion rate from H100-equivalents to average AGI company coders ηi, which is ηinit when initial automated, then increases at a rate of ηslope OOMs per effective compute OOM.

In order to combine automated coding labor with human coding labor, we use a CES that with parameter c controls the amount of serial coding labor. In particular, it sets to what extent coding is a fixed list of tasks that must always be done i.e. Amdahl’s law, vs. to what extent one can do more of the tasks that are automated in order to “substitute” for non-automated tasks.

Gi represents the average-human-equivalent labor assigned to task i. 𝝺 is a parallelization penalty used to translate from parallel to serial labor. LC,H,i represents the human labor assigned to task i and Caut,i represents the amount of H100-equivalents.

Model automation of experiment selection

The AIs’ experiment selection skill is measured in the same units as humans, i.e. roughly “value per experiment” that gets multiplied by the experiment throughput.

AIs’ research quality over time TAI(t) is defined based on their standard deviations relative to the frontier company human range TSD(t). TSD(t) increases linearly in terms of effective compute OOMs at a rate of Trate, following this post.[21]

For each human with worse experiment selection skill than the AI, raise their skill up to the level of the AI, then take the average of all of these values to get aggregate experiment selection skill T(E).
